There is a question that every computer system must ask, millions of times a day, to every user, every application, every service trying to access a resource.

*Who are you? And what are you allowed to do here?*

A deceptively simple question. But behind it lies an entire discipline, one that underpins the security, scalability, and reliability of almost every modern application: **Identity & Access Management**, or IAM.

## The problem, put simply

Picture a large company, Hundreds of employees. Dozens of applications accounting, HR, CRM, dev tooling, production servers. Every morning, people log in. Every evening, thye log out. In between, they access data, trigger processes, read documents.

Now, a few simple questions:
- How do you make sure the accountant can't access the production servers?
- How do you ensure a developer who leaves the company instantly loses all their access?
- How do you let an external partner reach one API without touching anything else?
- How do you know, six months later, who changed what and when?

Without a dedicated system, the answer to each of these questions is a patchwork of scripts, user tables duplicated across every app, passwords shared in Slack, and sleepless nights before security audits.

IAM is the structured answer to that chaos.

---

## What an IAM system actually does
An IAM system is the centralized gatekeeper of your infrastructure. It plays several roles at once.

#### The directory
It knows who exists. Every human user, every application, every machine service has a unique identity in the system. One place, one source of truth.


#### Authentication
It verifies you are who you claim to be. Password, second factor (TOTP, hardware security key), magic link via email, it orchestrates all of that. When you sign into an app using your Google or GitHub account, that's exactly this mechanism at work: Google is acting as the IAM for that app.

#### Authorization
Once your identity is confirmed, it decides what you're allowed to do. Read? Write? Delete? Administer? Permissions are defined through roles, assigned with precision based on your profile.

#### Standard protocols
A good IAM speaks the universal languages of the web, OAuth2 and OpenID Connect (OIDC). These standards allow an app to delegate authentication without ever touching user's passwords. They're what makes "Sign in with..." work everywhere.

#### Audit
It keeps a record of everything. Who logged in, from where, at what time, with what level of access. Essential for compliance, for security, for understanding what happened after an incident.

#### In short
IAM is what saves you from reinventing identity management in every new application you build.

## Why it's hard to get right

IAM sounds simple. It's just a `users` table with a `password_hash`, right?

No..

Managing identities seriously means dealing with dozens of problems that stack up fast: secret rotation, token revocation, session expiration, service accounts (those non-human identities representing apps or scripts), multi-tenancy (isolating different organizations within the same system), MFA with multiple possible strategies, webhooks to notify your systems on critical events, tamper-proof audit logs...

And all of this has to hold under load. Because an IAM is a mandatory chokepoint: every authenticated request goes through it. If the IAM is slow or goes down, your entire infrastructure stops.

That's why large enterprises pay fortunes for solutions like Okta, Auth0, or Azure AD. On the open source side, Keycloak has been around for a long time and does the job, but it carries significant configuration complexity and a JVM footprint that doesn't suit everyone.

FerrisKey doesn't claim to be the definitive solution. It's an alternative, with different trade-offs: Rust instead of Java, a hexagonal architecture designed for clarity and maintainability, and an ambition to stay simple to operate.

## Welcome to FerrisKey

FerrisKey is an open source IAM system, built in **Rust**, with a hexagonal architecture.

Why Rust? Because performance and reliability are non-negotiable for a component this critical. Rust delivers C-level speed without the pitfalls, no garbage collector, no data races, no memory surprises. For a service handling millions of authentications, that's exactly what you want.

The hexagonal architecture ensures that bsuiness logic stays pure and decoupled from infrastructure. The database can chance, the HTTP framework can evolve, the domain core stays stable and testable.

In practice, FerrisKey gives you:
- **Realms** : fully isolated spaces. Each organization, project, or environment can have its own realm with its own users, roles, and clients. No cross-contamination possible.
- **OAuth2 & OIDC** : the web's standards, implemented correctly, so your apps can delegate authentication without friction.
- **Trident** : FerisKey's MFA module, supporting TOTP, WebAuthn (hardware keys, passkeys), magic links, and recovery codes.
- **SeaWatch**: a complete audit system, queryable from the console, tracing every security events.
- **Webhooks** : to connect FerrisKey to your existing systems and react to lifecycle events (user creation, access revocation, etc).
- **Cloud-native** : an official Helm chart, Kubernetes-ready, for frictionless production deployment.

FerrisKey is licensed under **Apache 2.0**. No paywall. No "enterprise" tier hiding critical features behind a contact form. The code is open, the community is here.
